The dawn of security tokens...

by Arifa Khan - October 26, 2018 5239 Views 0 Comment

A brief history of security tokens...

2017 - The crypto world was on fire! And just like in any other boom, we were seeing random crazy stuff. Random yacht parties with newly minted crypto-enthusiasts, a hurriedly put-together conference every week, an ICO breaking some record. Anyone recall the dwarf-throwing fun in Wolf of Wall Street? Such buoyancy had eluded me even in the best of my investment banking times! What's the most I can boast of as an ex-banker? An offsite party at the Atlantic Paradise at Bahamas, or gambling away at the Luxembourg casinos without a care? I would have mentioned ho-parties had Uber not made them so infamous last year!

Cut to 2018. ICOs are mostly under the radar, though the over $15 billion raised in 2018 have far surpassed the $5.5 billion raised by ICOs last year. And Institutional Investors have joined the party! But the party is boring. No crazy playboy stuff anymore. Whats still keeping the lights on? Security Tokens. So let's tell you a story of Security Tokens.

Security Tokens - The Origins

Security Tokens started to come into recognition by mainstream crypto investors in Dec 2017, a good 20 months since the first well known security token was launched on 30 April 2016. The famous Ethereum DAO, a self-administered crypto replica of a VC fund. Anyone with a smattering of Financial instruments would have recognised the DAO as a financial instrument and a security.

However, that distinction was not an overarching one for the crypto community since the blockchain was a tech enabler of all functions that only the big financial institutions were capable of earlier. So, if a group of friends gathered and decided to replicate a financial instrument from their own savings, instead of buying it off the shelf of a well known big financial institution, why should anyone stop them? Or so goes the reasoning of any crypto believer, not just the creators of DAO.

So far, so good. So the DAO went on to raise over $150 million in the 28 day funding window, and was also dissolved as soon as it was formed, following the infamous hack of $55million which till date remains a mystery.

Due to an overlooked bug which allowed an investor/subscriber to continue to withdraw funds from the DAO even after he had hit his limit of contribution (like if an ATM allowed you to withdraw, but failed to immediately update the system with your new balance thereby allowing you to withdraw more than the funds in your account) , the DAO suffered one of the first damages of smart contract insecurity arising out of faulty code. The Ethereum management took a call to contain the losses and not recognise this fraudster’s inflicted damage and reversed the string of actions that led to the hack. Since everything was virtual and determined by code, and thereafter validated by the governance of the code, it was possible to return the original contributions (in ether which had a positive non zero economic value at the time of the DAO) to all the contributors and dissolve the DAO arrangement, as if the hack never happened. Thus was born the ETC, as a fallout of crypto’s first ever hard fork.

No one in the crypto world saw the DAO as a security until the SEC decided to do a post mortem and declared it a security token on 25 July 2017. Because we were all busy reimagining the possibilities of crypto, and cared little about permissions. The logic would be that, if a bunch of friends are creating something, even if it is a substitute of a financial instrument, then why would anyone think of taking any permission from anyone other than those friends? Anyway DAO generated way more publicity about “Code being Law” and the governance of crypto protocols or lack thereof and the concentration of power to decide on the future of a protocol in the hands of a few, than if it breached the precincts of traditional finance necessitating permissions.

The publicity generated by the DAO and the Ethereum hard fork awakened crypto folks to the possibility of this creative fundraising mechanism, of a self administering smart contract for crowdfunding. Lo and Behold, The Initial Coin Offering (ICO) was born! Crypto entrepreneurs who had been developing blockchain enterprise applications, and were dependent on big clients to pay their bills, or offering training workshops to other big firms had now suddenly found a magic key to fund raising, but it was yet to be tested. It is great if you have friends and family to form the initial funding team, but what if you don’t and who will fund your dreams?

Much happened in 2016 as and some crypto entrepreneurs who had been working on various innovations such as StorJ, Cosmos, Golem were the first to experiment with ICOs in 2017. The fundraising was public and successful and how? The tokens started pouring in to each ICO (mostly bitcoin and ether as they were the leading and most prominent cryptocurrencies). So each ICO’s success caused the price of the underlying ether token to rise, as funders had to buy ether off the market to contribute to each ICO. Also a standard fund raising smart contract emerged, and thus was born the ERC 20 token which any crypto entrepreneur could use, including ironically many of Ethereum competitors such as EOS. Imagine a competitor using your platform to crowdfund successfully, and then taking on your own platform? It’s only possible in crypto. We are living in interesting times indeed.

Then 2017 happened, the annus mirabilis for Crypto.

And I authored the first ever crypto White Paper on Security Tokens in June 2017, as the crypto fever was in full swing. No one had imagined nor laid down the practicalities of tokenising the real financial instruments like stocks and bonds until my white paper.

Then we saw some Security Token ICOs emerge that went on to raise hundreds of millions of dollars towards Dec 2017, such as T Zero, Bankex, Polymath etc. Note that every single Security Token ICO came after my white paper, not before - with the exceptions of Blockchain Capital, The DAO etc which were bespoke security tokens, or tokens representing a share in VC funds to be more precise.

What is a Security Token?

All those white papers talked about marketplaces and wallets for security tokens, but it was left for the issuers to issue bespoke security tokens, or follow the same old Wall Street practices such as relying on investment banks or regulated entities to put their compliance stamp on such tokens. No one has obtained a license, nor created an infrastructure to issue security tokens thus far!

The Platform for Traditional Financial Securities

The platform for traditional financial securities (such as creation of stocks and bonds, and without the involvement of investment banks) such as I had talked about in my original white paper is still missing to date, even after an year from my publishing that detailed concept.

So I further elaborated on that concept and laid down the practical details of building out an automated securities creation and issuance platform in my subsequent white paper published in May 2018. (http://wolfgangflatow.com/himalaya)

Merely an year after I first invented the concept of tokenising Wall Street securities (without Wall Street), institutional capital is getting into crypto at an accelerating pace. Literally every big Wall Street incumbent is laying the ground for security tokens, and the space is about to get very hot and sweaty.

While on the one hand Yale Endowment Fund (one of the largest with $29 billion AUM) and several other University Endowments have made commitments to crypto exclusive funds, on the other hand all top Wall Street banks are making way for security tokens. Morgan Stanley, JP Morgan, Citi, TD Ameritrade are all preparing for the Security Token onslaught. Fidelity Digital Assets has made an announcement about institutional style custody solutions for crypto assets. Meanwhile, the early crypto successes such as Coinbase, Binance, Circle are all getting the licenses to issue security tokens in various jurisdictions.

How nice to be called right on this one!

Last year in 2017, when I went around the world educating the not so financially savvy masses on tokenising traditional financial securities, people clearly saw me as pioneer and inventor because no one had ever come up with that concept before me. These days though, we are one of the many Security Token players and I have to drop the fact that I invented the concept when people ask me how are we different from other security players. Another major difference is that, most other projects including the Wall Street ones will play by the same old rules keeping TTPs (Trusted Third Parties) intact in the system, whereas we aspire to be a decentralised project making Wall Street optional in the equation, thereby eliminating fees. We are creating an alternative infrastructure to create Initial Security Token Offerings ( as substitutes for IPOs and Bond offerings) which is a superior zero cost alternative to Wall Street. We won't be charging the advisory and intermediary fees, and therein lies our unique differentiating proposition.

Why is removing TTPs important?

Because if you keep TTPs intact, you are not doing anything different from what Wall Street does, so the ills of the current system - opacity, high threshold fees, precluding access to deserving entrepreneurs, biases of capitalism - are perpetuated in a supposedly decentralised protocol which is not democratising access to capital.

“Commercial security is a matter of solving the practical problems of business relationships such as privacy, integrity, protecting property, or detecting breach of contract. A security hole is any weakness that increases the risk of violating these goals. In this real world view of security, a problem does not dissapear because a designer assumes it away. The invocation or assumption in a security protocol design of a "trusted third party" (TTP) or a "trusted computing base" (TCB) controlled by a third party constitutes the introduction of a security hole into that design. The security hole will then need to be plugged by other means."

If the risks and costs of TTP institutional alternatives were not accounted for in the protocol design, the resulting protocol will in most cases be too costly or risky to be practical. If the protocol beats these odds and proves practical, it will only succeed after extensive effort has gone into plugging the TTP security hole(s). TTP assumptions cause most of the costs and risks in a security protocol, and plugging TTP security holes produces the most benefit and profit. As a result, we propose a security protocol design methodology whereby the most risky and expensive part(s) of a security protocol, the trusted third partie(s), are designed in parallel with security protocol(s) using those parties. The objectives of cost and risk minimization are focused on the TTPs rather than the security protocols themselves, which should be designed to suit the cost and risk minimized TTPs.” - Nick Szabo, originally published in 2001
