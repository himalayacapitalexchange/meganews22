the promise of smart securities

This Christmas is certainly unlike any other, for crypto folks atleast.

If the prolonged bear market has sapped your festivities of colour, let me try to give you a glimmer of hope and a few good things to come.

Whether you believe in cryptocurrencies or not, here is a new emerging industry you cannot ignore - Security Tokens. Because tokenisation of real world securities such as shares and bonds have serious benefits over their current counterparts.

Do emerging technologies hold the potential to displace law?

This may sound too futuristic to many to be real, but some computer science researchers certainly believed so in the early 1990s, which laid the whole premise for smart contracts.

Nick Szabo was the harbinger of this notion that machines would enforce agreements between entities or humans, when he proposed and coined the term 'smart contracts'.

Though smart contracts have long existed in the form of Coke vending machines, Ticket vending machines (earliest example of smart contracts as envisaged by Nick), their scope was limited by physical terrain. Meaning that you had to physically interact with the machine, so be in close proximity, for the mechanised contract to work. Also, the law enforcement problem was taken care of by the economics. No man was destitute or hungry enough to call upon the wrath of law enforcement by breaking the machine, for a few coke bottles. If you had a gold vending machine, it would be a different story though!

(Despair not, I have not suddenly turned a coloured water salesman. No, not yet! I wouldn't ever risk turning Steve Jobs in his grave. I imagine being Steve's friend in a parallel universe!)

When the agreement required commercial interaction between parties-not-present (such as someone not physically present at the coke machine), the contract coalesced to simple e-commerce, where a trusted third party (TTP) such as a payment provider, VISA, Paypal was required to enforce agreements and mediate disputes.

It is only with the advent of bitcoin and cryptographically definitive transfer of value that smart contracts have broadened in scope to include geographically dispersed parties - aka - the internet strangers.

So, smart contracts are a marriage between electronic agreements and cryptography.

Read A Brief History on Smart Contracts by Arifa Khan here.

Since we started the discussion with the question of whether smart contracts can substitute for law, let's explore more.

The difference between Smart Contracts and Contract Law:

"Contract law is a remedial institution. It's aim is not to ensure performance ex-ante, but to adjudicate the grievances that may arise ex-post. Smart contracts eliminate the act of remediation by eliminating the possibility of breach". - Kevin Werbach, Wharton School of Business & Nicolas Cornell, University of Michigan Law School

Not only that, smart contracts are also very different from normal contracts that we see in the business world as they are so complex that currently only the computer and blockchain literate are able to decipher and interpret the code to translate to everyday agreement or business logic therein. We can envisage the steps as:

- Business Agreement
- Logic underlying the agreement
- Translate to Code
- Performance of Code in real life as intended

This is another Pandora's box when it comes to code competence and code integrity, which drives the importance of Smart Contract Audit these days.

Another research stemming from my alma mater University of Pennsylvania again, analysed 100+ ICOs on code competence and code integrity and published some rather disturbing revelations. Anyway, such shocks have been well reflected in current crypto prices, one would reckon, though the bad apples have plausibly spoilt the market for all, atleast for the time being.

Reference: Coin Operated Capitalism by Shaanan Cohney, David Hoffman, Jeremy Sklaroff and David Wishnick

Simplifying Smart Contracts for the Common Man - The Role of AI

Some Artificial Intelligence companies are working on adding natural language processing features to smart contract code creation so that code illiterate users can also custom create smart contracts - this would be a real driver of the mass adoption and smart contract enabled disruption, as they are simply out of the reach of common man today. In this new paradigm, users can give commands in English or Mandarin that the machine would then translate to code.

This is how a natural language based smart contract synthesis framework would look:

"The idea is that a user defines an intention for her transaction in a natural language (English). The speech will be processed by a language recognition software to extract the text. Then a deep neural network based program identifies the key factors of the transaction which are sent to a program synthesis framework for code generation. The final output is an executable program for the smart contract as well as an equivalent flow graph for user confirmation."

Somehow, Vitalik Buterin missed this step of dumbing down his genius idea so that it would truly penetrate the economic and business realms like Microsoft and Apple did. Therefore, like in all other tech revolutions, a user design, user interface and user interaction revolution is afoot in the blockchain world too.

We are always looking for AI partners with an edge, and endeavour to find such avant grade companies and present them at Fintech Storm conclaves such as the upcoming AI Crypto Showcase in London 8 January 2019.

Can a Smart Contract represent ownership?

As long as code can convey 3 features namely verifiability, transferability, accruing value, it can convey ownership.

So, what sort of transactions can be represented by smart contracts?

- Payments
- Remittances
- Crowd sourcing
- Crowd funding
- Creation of value such as in financial securities
- Distribution of value such as in dividends
- Collaboration between multiple parties subject to binding agreements such as in syndication of loans
- Creation of new instruments and securities hitherto unimagined..

What would that mean in the context of Smart Securities?

The worldwide democratisation of capital raising is unprecedented.
Uber and Lyft, the global ride hailing companies are rushing now for gigantic IPOs, with global economic uncertainty looming large.
That is the public's last and only chance to partake in the success stories of these giants - Uber and Lyft, and yes after the venture capitalists , banks, financial institutions and privileged wealthy companies have already gotten their lion's share of returns from the growth stories.
If you are not of the scale of Uber & Lyft, despair not. You can still fundraise through legally compliant securities (not ICOs) thanks to the advent of blockchain, and specifically to smart securities. Notwithstanding the ICO bear market that is.
Why?
Because, regardless of what the future holds for cryptocurrencies, the blockchain has brought a real innovation to capital markets - and that is to raise capital through tokenised securities.
Tokenised Securities promise better liquidity, easier access to investor universe, more efficient processes of ownership transfer by eliminating settlements layers, more efficient investor relationships, greater transparency, more democratic and overall a superior system to current wall street machine. And if you take the game farther, to really where it should take aim, then no or little fees to pay to any of the intermediaries or investment banks.
The world of capital raising is certainly gearing up for some permanent changes.
Security Tokens industry is estimated to cover a mind boggling USD500 trillion in a decade's time, and we are at ground zero! An unprecedented investment of USD500 million has already gone into security token ecosystem in 2018 alone. However, most of the USD500 million investments have gone into companies purporting to play in bespoke security tokens (think tokens representing gold coins, tokens representing trees, tokens representing ownership in a VC fund, tokens representing units of ownership of a luxury condominium, tokens offering percentage of revenues or profits etc..) and none so far in the eco system of smart securities, which are the precinct of Wall Street.
However, the security token ecosystem is certainly alive and kicking, with a lot of regulatory certainty emerging (favourable ton the ecosystem) , and several players (both from the traditional Wall Street background and successful crypto pioneers such as crypto exchanges) are busy laying the groundwork for this upcoming wave. Several of these players got together recently at a New Zealand resort called Millbrook and formed a "Hillbrook Accord" - a new open source verified token framework to provide voluntary industry guidelines on making security tokens interoperable.
But, who is creating these tokens in the first place? Where are the infrastructure rails? Are they expecting to rely on older Wall Street models for token creation? Because mind you, smart securities are just financial securities legally (though all security tokens may not be replicas of financial securities).
